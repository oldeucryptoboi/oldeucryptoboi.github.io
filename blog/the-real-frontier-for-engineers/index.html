The Real Frontier for Engineers

Software EngineeringAIFirst PrinciplesFuture of Engineering

AI can recombine what already exists. The interesting work is the problems where no pattern exists yet.

A few years ago I was working on software projects that took years before reaching production. Not weeks or months. Years. The work was valuable and technically challenging, and the teams involved cared deeply about getting things right. Still, after spending long stretches of time on systems like that, I started asking myself a simple question: what is actually the next frontier for engineers?

At first I assumed the answer was straightforward. Build bigger systems, ship more features, make existing platforms faster and more reliable. That has always been a big part of engineering work. But the more I thought about it, the more I realized that the most interesting problems weren’t necessarily the ones where the path was already known. The work that really pulled me in was different. It was the problems where the solution didn’t exist yet, where there was no tutorial, no StackOverflow thread, and sometimes not even a clear roadmap pointing in the right direction.

First principles over known patterns

Those are the situations where you have to slow down and think from first principles. You experiment, try ideas that fail, step back, and try again. Sometimes progress is messy and uncertain, but that process is exactly what makes engineering interesting in the first place. You’re not just implementing a known pattern. You’re figuring something out.

This is also why I find the conversation about AI replacing developers a bit incomplete. Large language models are incredibly powerful tools, and I use them regularly. They can generate code, speed up development, and help solve problems that already have established patterns. But that strength comes from recombining knowledge that already exists. When a problem has been solved many times before, AI can often produce a solution faster than we can.

Don’t compete with AI. Redirect.

So if AI can write that kind of code for you, maybe the right move isn’t to compete with it. Maybe the better move is to let it handle those tasks and shift our attention elsewhere.

The real frontier for engineers may not be writing routine code faster. It may be moving toward the problems that don’t have existing patterns yet. The ones where there is no clear answer and where imagination, experimentation, and deep thinking are required to move forward. Those are the problems where humans still have an advantage.

AI can help generate solutions based on what it has seen before. Humans can imagine solutions to problems that have never been solved before. That distinction matters more than ever.

Curiosity as a competitive advantage

Tools will continue to improve, and AI will undoubtedly become a bigger part of how we build software. But curiosity, creativity, and first-principles thinking remain at the core of engineering. If anything, those qualities become more valuable as tools handle more of the routine work.

That’s the kind of work that excites me the most today. The problems where the answer isn’t obvious yet, and where figuring it out requires thinking beyond the patterns that already exist.
